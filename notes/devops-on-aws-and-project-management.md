## Module 1: Defining the Project

### What is a Project

**Characteristics of a Project**
- A project is temporary and unique, with a specific goal and a clear end date, which can vary in length.
- It has a defined start and finish, with timelines that may be adjusted but must be communicated clearly.

**Defining Deliverables and Constraints**
- Deliverables must be specific and measurable, such as reducing a cost by a certain percentage within a set timeframe.
- Constraints include budget, resources, and risks, which must be identified to avoid project failure.

**Identifying Stakeholders**
- Stakeholders are individuals or organizations involved in or affected by the project.
- Defining stakeholders helps assign tasks properly and ensures the project outcomes meet their needs.

### Project Management Best Practices

**Project Justification and Planning**
- Justifying a project involves assessing costs, duration, benefits, and opportunity costs to ensure viability and alignment with organizational goals.
- Creating a project summary with SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound) helps clarify objectives and success criteria.

**Project Plan Development**
- A project plan outlines milestones, task dependencies, time estimates, and resource assignments to guide execution and track progress.
- Tools like Gantt charts visually represent tasks, dependencies, assignments, and completion status for effective monitoring.

**Execution and Retrospective**
- Project plans must be flexible to accommodate unexpected changes, with ongoing communication and stakeholder check-ins to stay on track.
- After project completion, a retrospective reviews what worked, what needs improvement, and lessons learned to improve future projects.

### Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)

The Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is a structured process that ensures the right people are involved at the right stages to build an application that meets business goals and requirements.

**Planning and Analysis**
- This phase involves gathering the team and stakeholders to define the problem, objectives, and feasibility regarding time, resources, and budget.
- It ensures alignment on what needs to be built and sets the foundation for the project.

**Design**
- Focuses on the technical aspects such as the software environment, programming languages, and architecture including microservices.
- Responsibilities for different parts of the application are clearly defined and communicated.

**Implementation and Testing**
- Implementation is where actual coding and infrastructure setup happen, involving developers, system administrators, and database administrators.
- Testing involves quality assurance to verify that the application works correctly, covering all parts thoroughly to avoid issues in production.

**Deployment and Maintenance**
- Deployment starts with staging environments for final testing and business validation before going live in production.
- Maintenance is ongoing, addressing bugs, customer issues, and performance monitoring, with major changes restarting the SDLC process.

**Process Flexibility**
- The SDLC phases can overlap and are not strictly linear; parts of the application can be tested while others are still in development.
- The SDLC serves as a guiding framework rather than rigid rules, allowing adaptation to ensure progress without unnecessary delays.

### SDLC Best Practices

**Planning and Analysis**
- Engage product owners and end users early to define functionality and ensure the product meets real needs.
- Use nonworking mockups to communicate project goals clearly without technical jargon.

**Software Design**
- Make architectural and technology choices considering long-term support and team skills.
- Use "software spikes" (small experimental coding) to validate technical approaches before full implementation.

**Implementation**
- Organize coding efforts with source control to enable collaboration and code sharing.
- Establish coding standards and practice continuous integration with automated builds to maintain code quality.

**Documentation and Testing**
- Provide just enough documentation to help new team members and support engineers understand the project.
- Conduct both functional testing (verifying features) and nonfunctional testing (performance, usability, security), tracking bugs systematically.

**Deployment and Maintenance**
- Automate deployments to reduce human error and enable frequent, smaller updates for faster feedback.
- Prioritize bug fixes and write maintainable code to support long-term product stability and ease of troubleshooting.
